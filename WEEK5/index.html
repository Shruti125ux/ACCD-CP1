Project Overview

Challenge: Creative freelancers juggle multiple projects while constantly on the move. They need quick access to their Notion workspace without being tethered to their desktops or laptops.

Solution: Redesign the Notion mobile app specifically for creative freelancers who need to check progress, manage tasks, and stay organized while working from cafes, co-working spaces, or outdoor environments.

Approach: Understand the physical environments where users interact with the app, then design contextually-aware mobile experiences that fit seamlessly into their workflow.

Phase 1: Environmental Exploration

Understanding User Context

As a designer with an architectural background, I believe that understanding the physical environment is crucial to designing effective digital experiences. Before diving into the app redesign, I explored the three primary environments where creative freelancers work when they're away from their primary workstations.

This environmental research helps inform decisions about interface brightness, interaction patterns (one-handed vs. two-handed), notification strategies, and feature prioritization based on context.

Three Key Environments Explored

1. Cafe / Coffee House

Context: Social atmosphere, ambient noise, natural and ambient lighting, often shared seating

User Behavior: Quick check-ins, light editing, responding to comments, reviewing progress

Design Implications: Need for glanceable information, easy one-handed navigation, distraction-resistant interface

2. Home Workspace / Desk

Context: Controlled environment, good lighting, minimal distractions, comfortable seating

User Behavior: Longer sessions, detailed work, focused task completion, reference checking

Design Implications: Support for extended mobile use, detailed view options, comfortable reading interfaces

3. Outdoor Creative Space

Context: Natural light (potentially bright sunlight), varying seating, inspiration-seeking

User Behavior: Ideation, capturing quick notes, brainstorming, checking deadlines

Design Implications: High contrast for sunlight readability, quick capture features, voice input support

Key Insight: While all three environments have unique characteristics, the cafe/coffee house emerged as the most common and versatile workspace for creative freelancers. It balances social energy with productivity, offers amenities (power, wifi, coffee), and provides a change of scenery that many freelancers crave.

Phase 2: Deep Dive into Cafe Environment

After exploring all three environments, I chose to focus specifically on the cafe/coffee house context for detailed design exploration. This environment represents the quintessential "mobile freelancer" experience and offers rich opportunities for contextual design.

Object Mapping & Ecosystem Analysis

Cafe Environment: Visual Research

Explored material palettes, lighting conditions, and spatial arrangements typical of coffee houses. Notice the warm wood tones, ambient lighting from Edison bulbs, natural materials, and the balance between private focus and social awareness.

User Ecosystem Mapping

Identified all objects and touchpoints in a creative freelancer's cafe environment. Central to the experience is the freelancer surrounded by essential tools and environmental elements:

  • Digital Tools: Mobile phone, laptop (not always), work diary/planner
  • Physical Comforts: Coffee cup, donut/snack, comfortable seating (table, chair)
  • Ambient Elements: Lighting (natural and artificial), plants (atmosphere), pen (note-taking)
  • Personal Items: Purse/bag (mobility and security)

This mapping helps prioritize features: quick glanceability (competing with coffee, conversations), one-handed operation (other hand holds coffee), and contextual notifications that don't disrupt the cafe flow.

Design Methodology

Human-Centered Design Approach

My architectural training emphasized understanding how people inhabit and move through space. I'm applying these same principles to mobile UX design:

  • Spatial Context: Physical environment shapes digital interaction patterns
  • Ergonomics: How users hold phones, reach for coffee, maintain posture affects UI layout
  • Lighting Design: Environmental lighting conditions inform color choices and contrast ratios
  • User Flow: Like circulation in architecture, information flow must feel natural and intuitive
  • Materiality: Digital affordances should feel as tangible as physical materials
